Debunking Happiness Myths

Learn the truth about what really makes us happy.

Common Myths About Happiness

Professor Santos's course reveals that many of our intuitions about happiness are wrong. For instance, people often believe that material wealth, high achievements, or significant life changes will bring lasting happiness. However, research shows that these factors have minimal impact on long-term well-being.

Santos introduces the concept of the "G.I. Joe Fallacy," which suggests that knowing what makes us happy is not enough to achieve happiness. Instead, we must actively practice behaviors that lead to happiness, such as gratitude, optimism, and physical activity.

In the anthology This Idea Must Die: Scientific Theories That Are Blocking Progress, Laurie Santos and Tamar Gendler, a philosophy professor at Yale, contributed an essay titled "Knowing Is Half the Battle." They introduce the concept of the "G.I. Joe Fallacy," named after the old G.I. Joe cartoon that used the phrase "Knowing is half the battle." Santos and Gendler argue that this phrase is misleading. According to recent cognitive science research, knowing is actually a very small part of the battle for most real-world decisions. For example, while people know that $19.99 is almost the same as $20.00, the former still feels like a significantly better deal.

The course begins with the understanding that our minds are adept at convincing us to follow intuitions about happiness that are completely incorrect. To illustrate, Santos presents a common test: if a baseball and a bat together cost $1.10, and the bat costs one dollar more than the ball, how much does the ball cost? The intuitive answer is ten cents, but the correct answer is five cents. If our brains can be easily tricked by simple arithmetic, imagine how easily they can mislead us about our own well-being.

Now, think of a list of things that you believe would make you happier. These can be significant changes (a raise, moving to a new city, a new partner) or minor ones (a treat from a vending machine).

Finished your list? Let’s review. Nearly everything you listed is likely to be wrong. Most of what people believe will make them happier — assuming basic needs are met — are circumstantial changes: more money, a different home or job, a long vacation, or a snack. Our minds constantly tell us that obtaining these things will make us happy. However, science shows this belief is incorrect. The real reasons why are covered in Lecture No. 2.

Myth vs. Reality

Myth #1: Circumstances are the Key to Happiness

While it's easy to assume that our external situations, such as wealth and status, are the main contributors to our happiness, research shows that these factors have the smallest impact once our basic needs are met.

Myth #2: Changing Circumstances Leads to Happiness

People often believe that altering their circumstances will significantly enhance their happiness. However, studies indicate that changing our behaviors has a much greater effect on our well-being than changing our circumstances.

Myth:#3 Happiness Comes from Achieving Milestones

It's a common misconception that reaching certain milestones or acquiring material goods will bring lasting happiness. True happiness stems from habits like spending time with loved ones, practicing gratitude, staying optimistic, being physically active, and enjoying life's moments.
